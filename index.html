The Technique

Physical Holography:
Light Captured in Layers

This installation achieves a holographic three-dimensional effect entirely through material physics. Large transparent Mylar sheets, each 36×48 inches, are individually printed with a specific architectural "slice" extracted from a computational model. Each printed Mylar sheet is then meticulously adhered to a matching plexiglass panel, creating a rigid, optically clear substrate for the image.

These panels are arranged in a precise stack on a low pedestal, separated by 2-inch air gaps that provide physical depth between each layer. The total assembly reaches approximately 10 inches deep, creating a tangible volumetric space in which the image exists.

The transformative element is light. COB (Chip on Board) LED strips, concealed at the base of the structure, project illumination upward through the edges of each plexiglass panel. The plexiglass acts as a waveguide, channeling light along its surface until it encounters the printed ink on the Mylar. At these contact points, the light scatters and glows, causing the architectural forms to appear luminous and suspended in mid-air.

Assembly Process

From Data to Light

STEP 01

Computational Slicing

The source architectural form is computationally divided into 10 discrete cross-sectional slices, each representing a specific depth plane within the volumetric model.

STEP 02

Mylar Printing

Each slice is printed onto large-format transparent Mylar sheets at 36×48 inches, preserving the color fidelity and tonal gradients of the original computational rendering.

STEP 03

Plexiglass Lamination

Printed Mylar sheets are meticulously adhered to optically clear plexiglass panels of matching dimensions, creating rigid substrates that also serve as light guides.

STEP 04

Spatial Assembly

Panels are stacked with precision-cut 2-inch spacers on a low pedestal, reconstructing the volumetric form as a physical depth-field across the 10-inch assembly.

STEP 05

Edge Illumination

COB LED strips are installed at the base, projecting light upward into the plexiglass edges. Light propagates through the panels via total internal reflection until it hits printed ink, producing the luminous floating effect.

The Experience
“The internal architectural forms glow and float in mid-air, creating a shimmering, high-tech vision of Architectural Intelligence that transforms as you walk around it.”

The parallax effect between layers shifts with the viewer's position, causing the sculpture to reveal different aspects of its internal geometry from every angle. The piece exists somewhere between physical object and projected image—a data portrait made tangible through light and material.

The Artist

Natasha Bajc

Natasha Bajc defines art as a "meeting with the Real as a nervous system bypass"—a visceral provocation that strips away the intellectual safety of language to engage the human body at its most primal level.

As a cancer survivor and expert in resilience, Bajc's work is a profound autopsy of biological endurance. For Bajc, resilience is not a psychological mindset but a somatic event: the capacity to endure the raw, un-named weight of existence without retreating into the safety of fantasy.
